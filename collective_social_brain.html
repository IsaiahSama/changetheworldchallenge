The Collective Social Brain Hypothesis

The Collective Social Brain hypothesis posits that individuals tend to fall into two distinct groups regarding their reasoning styles and perspectives on social protection and provision.

One group identifies with vulnerable populations and believes that the solution to problems related to social protection is increasing rights and protections provided by society. This group often sees issues as outside of their control and uses Type I (intuitive) reasoning. Type I reasoning relies on pattern recognition and consensus building, which is effective for solving problems where solutions are uncomputable and rely on past experiences.

The other group tends not to identify as vulnerable and believes the solution to these problems lies in taking more personal responsibility for protection and provision. This group tends to use Type II (logical) reasoning, which involves methodical and computable processes. This type of reasoning is more effective for problems that require logical deduction and where the correct solution lies within a minority of perspectives.

Reasoning Styles and Social Polarization

These two reasoning types tend to come to different conclusions based on the same information. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic, individuals who felt vulnerable were biased toward stronger lockdown measures and government intervention, while those who felt personally responsible prioritized personal freedom. Both groups viewed their conclusions as "logical" based on their respective reasoning styles.

Consensus-driven individuals (Type I) may disregard information not supported by expert consensus, while logical individuals (Type II) may ignore consensus in favor of independent logical reasoning. This polarization can lead to conflicting perspectives on issues like public health, social justice, and governance.

Implications for Group Decision-Making

The Collective Social Brain hypothesis highlights the difficulty of switching between these two reasoning styles in larger groups, which may lead to polarization rather than effective decision-making. In small, tightly-knit groups, mechanisms for switching between intuitive and logical reasoning exist, but in large modern societies, such mechanisms are often absent.

A General Collective Intelligence (GCI) platform, which facilitates decision-making at the group level, could provide a mechanism for switching between reasoning styles and thus increase the problem-solving capacity of larger groups. GCI is predicted to exponentially enhance group decision-making, with profound implications for addressing global challenges like poverty and climate change.
